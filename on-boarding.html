Expectations

  • Cho Lab Ten Commandments
  • Time: As long as you overlap “core hours” (4-5 hr window anywhere between 10-4 pm) with me and other people in the lab, I am okay with you choosing your own schedule. I am usually in 10-6. If you worked a full day on Sat or Sun, feel free to take a day off during the week.
  • Email: Unless it is something really urgent, I do not expect you to check or respond after hours. I also do not expect you to reply to my FYI or housekeeping emails, but very short acknowledgement (“Ack.”) or reacting using thumbs-up or other emojis on Outlook would be appreciated.
  • Lab notebook: You are free to choose physical vs electronic (Benchling, LabArchives, Evernote, OneNote, Bear, …). However, if you go digital, please print out your notes every month.
  • Quality-over-quantity: One thorough, conclusive experiment is better than two hard-to-interpret plus two failed experiments. Always have controls that can tell you what went wrong. (As you might already know, in scientific research, if you have a suspision that something will go wrong or will not work, it will.) I try do experiments only when I can fully focus – not in a rush, not hungry but not full, well hydrated, feel sharp enough to handle unexpected, etc.
  • ”Failed” experiment: Experiments fail for many reasons – pipetting mistake, old reagents, instrument glitches, wrong temperature/concentration, etc – and that is completely normal. No need to hide them. Just tell me (1) what you think might have gone wrong and (2) how you plan to troubleshoot, and we take it from there. It is okay to be down for a while, but after a short grieving period, plus two chocolate bars and your favorite drink, I expect you to bounce back. This resilience is the single most important quality that I look for in my lab members.
  • Paper reading: 1-2 yrs after joining the lab, you have to be more knowledgeable than me when it comes to your project. Please corner me: “You didn’t read THAT paper?!” I also want you to be organized – use a reference manager software or develop your own system (folder-based, spreadsheet-based, etc) that works. If you are spending more than 1 min ⏰ to pull up the paper that you want to read/check again, you need a new system.
  • Meeting slides: I expect you to make high-quality 1-on-1 and lab mtg slides. Preparing figures/slides/talks forces you to think deeply, and it is okay to spend extra time making them look super-clear, aesthetic, and important (but don’t oversell). One way to think about this: You spent days and weeks to generate that data – blood, sweat, and tears! You want to make your final product, i.e., figures/slides/talks, look as good as possible.

Generative AI guideline

  • As a society, we're still figuring out how best to use generative AI tools like ChatGPT. Because things are evolving quickly, these guidelines will be updated regularly.
  • Manuscript/Grant proposal writing: It is acceptable to use generative AI to refine sentence structure, check grammar, and adjust tone. However, I do not recommend relying on AI to generate paragraphs containing scientific claims. As of mid-2025, AI struggles to ‘synthesize’ knowledge like humans, and seems to rely heavily on a limited set of highly cited sources.
  • Coding: It's okay to use generative AI for coding, but be sure to carefully review the entire code and understand all functions and commands. Check that edge cases are properly handled. Note that generative AI sometimes suppress errors – such as by using try-except blocks in Python – without fully resolving the underlying issues. It is recommended to break down problems into smaller tasks and have AI assist with each part, rather than asking it to write the entire code from scratch.
